Slide 2 - Foundation

What Is Quantum Computing?

Definition

Quantum computing is a new paradigm of computation that leverages the principles of quantum mechanics, superposition, entanglement, and interference, to process information.

Core Concept

  • Classical computers use bits: 0 or 1.
  • Quantum computers use qubits that can exist in multiple states simultaneously.
  • This expands computational possibilities beyond classical logic paths.

Deep Insight

  • Classical computing: deterministic logic.
  • Quantum computing: probabilistic amplitude manipulation.
  • Information is encoded in quantum states of particles like electrons or photons.

Slide 4 - Mechanics

How Quantum Computers Work

1) Qubits

Physical implementations include:

  • Superconducting circuits
  • Trapped ions
  • Photonic systems
  • Topological qubits

2) Superposition

|ψ⟩ = α|0⟩ + β|1⟩, where α and β are probability amplitudes.

3) Entanglement: qubits become correlated beyond classical limits.

4) Quantum Gates: Hadamard, CNOT, and Pauli-X manipulate amplitudes.

Quantum circuits manipulate probability amplitudes through interference to amplify correct answers.

Quantum Circuit (H + CNOT)

Signal pulses represent quantum state propagation through the circuit.

Slide 6 - Superposition

Superposition Explained

Coin Analogy

Classical bit: coin on table (Heads or Tails).

Qubit: spinning coin (Heads and Tails at once).

Deeper Concept

  • Measurement collapses the state.
  • Until measured, the qubit exists as a wave function.
  • This is not classical randomness, it is quantum probability amplitude.

Slide 7 - Entanglement

Quantum Entanglement

Definition

A physical phenomenon where two particles become linked such that the state of one instantly determines the state of the other, regardless of distance.

Importance

Einstein called it:

"Spooky action at a distance."

  • Quantum teleportation
  • Quantum cryptography
  • Massive computational speedups

Entanglement is the backbone of quantum advantage.

Slide 11 - Challenges

Challenges

Decoherence

Quantum states collapse due to environmental interference.

Error Rates

Quantum gates are noisy, and reliability remains a major challenge.

Cryogenic Requirements

Most systems operate near absolute zero to preserve qubit coherence.

Scalability

Maintaining thousands or millions of stable qubits is extremely difficult.

We are currently in the NISQ Era (Noisy Intermediate-Scale Quantum).
